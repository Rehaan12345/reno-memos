Process Overview 
Pursuant to Council’s request and in preparation for the Fiscal Year 2026/27 budget cycle, staff 
prepared a regional fee comparison of the City’s adopted Fiscal Year 2025/26 fees and Washoe 
County, the City of Sparks, and Carson City fees. This information is provided in advance of the 
FY26/27 budget workshops to support Council’s understanding of the City’s fee structure within 
a regional context and to inform broader discussions related to long-term financial planning and 
service delivery. 
The City’s budget development process begins each year with departmental planning during 
November and December, with an emphasis on understanding Council priorities, assessing 
operational sustainability, and maintaining financial stability. As part of this process, fee 
schedules are reviewed annually to evaluate whether they continue to align with service costs, 
policy objectives, and community expectations. Regular fee review allows Council to consider 
whether fees for services should fully recover costs, partially recover costs, or be subsidized by 
the General Fund. 
The attached document (Attachment A – FY25/26 Regional Fee Schedule Comparison) provides 
a comprehensive overview of the City’s current fee schedule and presents corresponding fees 
from neighboring jurisdictions where comparable services exist. In cases where a direct 
comparison is not available, “N/A” or similar indicators are used to reflect differences in service 
offerings or fee structures.  

 
 
Key Insights and Usefulness of Regional Comparison 
The purpose of this comparison is to provide transparency and context regarding how the City’s 
fees relate to those of surrounding agencies, recognizing that alignment, cost recovery, and 
equity considerations may differ by jurisdiction. Regional benchmarking can help identify broad 
trends, areas of relative alignment, and categories where fee-setting approaches diverge due to 
policy or operational choices. 
While regional comparisons do not establish a single “correct” fee level, they provide useful 
reference points that can inform discussions related to competitiveness, cost recovery 
philosophy, and service delivery expectations. 
Interpretation Guidance 
Direct comparison of municipal fees across the region is inherently challenging due to significant 
differences in how agencies structure, calculate, and apply fees. The City’s fee schedule is more 
granular and detailed than those of some regional partners, with specific fees tied to defined 
services or license types. For example, the City assesses distinct business license fees by activity, 
while other jurisdictions rely more heavily on gross-receipts-based models. While this level of 
detail can support accurate cost recovery, it can also increase administrative complexity and 
make side-by-side comparisons less straightforward. 
Additional challenges arise from differences in fee methodologies, including the use of flat fees, 
valuation-based fees, hourly rates, or a combination of approaches. Jurisdictions also vary in 
whether plan review, inspections, and administrative costs are bundled into a single fee or 
assessed separately. As a result, similar projects may experience different total costs depending 
on project characteristics, review complexity, and local practices, even when published fees 
appear higher or lower. 
Regional comparisons are further influenced by variations in service levels, staffing models, 
regulatory frameworks, adopted codes, and local policy objectives. In some cases, an agency 
with lower published fees may offset costs through greater General Fund support, while others 
may rely more heavily on user fees to fund service delivery. Consequently, regional fee 
comparisons are best understood as contextual benchmarks rather than direct equivalency 
assessments. 
Looking Forward 
Fees are generally designed to offset the cost of providing fee-supported services, including 
personnel, overhead, technology, and regulatory compliance. Personnel-related costs such as 
salaries, benefits, training, and overtime, along with expenses for software, professional services, 
materials, and fuel, continue to affect service delivery costs. Many fee-supported activities, 
including plan review, inspections, and enforcement, also contribute directly to public safety and 
regulatory compliance, making adequate resourcing an important consideration. 
Regular, annual review of the fee schedule provides Council with an opportunity to understand 
the long-term sustainability of fee-supported programs, set cost recovery or subsidization goals, 
 
 
and avoid the need for infrequent, larger adjustments. This approach supports transparent 
financial planning and informed decision-making while recognizing the operational and policy 
factors that influence municipal fee structures. 
Clear policy direction from Council regarding cost recovery versus subsidization goals is an 
important component of the annual fee review and budget development process. Establishing 
whether specific services are intended to fully recover costs, partially recover costs, or remain 
subsidized by the General Fund provides a consistent framework for staff analysis and 
recommendations. This direction helps guide staff as they evaluate fee schedules, assess fiscal 
impacts, and develop options for consideration during upcoming budget workshops. Clarifying 
these policy objectives in advance supports transparent decision-making and ensures that future 
recommendations align with Council priorities and broader financial planning goals.
